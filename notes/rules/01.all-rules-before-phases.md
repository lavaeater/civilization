# I. Overview

## 1. Introduction

**1.1** ADVANCED CIVILIZATION is a game of skill for two to eight players which covers the development of ancient civilizations from the invention of agriculture to the emergence of Rome as the dominant Mediterranean power — a span of almost 8,000 years. Each player leads a nation of peoples over a mapboard of the Mediterranean and Near East as they attempt to carve a niche for themselves and their culture.

**1.2** Although battles and territorial strategy are important, ADVANCED CIVILIZATION is not a war game because it is not won by battle or conquest. Instead, the object of play is to gain a level of overall advancement involving cultural, economic, political and religious factors. Conflicts which arise result from rivalries and shortages of land rather than attempts to eliminate other players.

**1.3** A game of ADVANCED CIVILIZATION can take up to ten or twelve hours to complete. When this much time is not available, players should play to a fixed time limit (see 34.1B).

---

## 2. Description of Play

**2.1** The object is to acquire the greatest number of victory points (35). The winner will not necessarily be the first player to reach the end of the Archaeological Succession Track (A.S.T.) or the player with the greatest number of civilization card points, although these are important factors.

**2.2** Occupancy of areas is indicated by tokens (groups of peoples) and cities. Each turn, each player increases his population by adding one token to each area containing a single token and two tokens to each area containing two or more tokens.

**2.3** Players may then move tokens by land or by boarding ships and moving by sea into other land areas.

**2.4** After movement, conflict is resolved in land areas with surplus population.

**2.5** After conflict, cities may be constructed in areas with enough tokens. Cities resist attack better and generate revenue through taxes (tokens moved from stock to treasury).

**2.6** Each turn, players collect one trade card for each city they have on the board. Most are commodities; some are calamities. After trading, commodity sets and tokens in transit may be exchanged for civilization cards.

**2.7** Cities provide wealth for civilization. A nation that fails to augment wealth through trade will be surpassed by others.

**2.8** Civilization cards confer special abilities and immunities and provide the bulk of points needed to progress along the A.S.T. Credits from less expensive cards reduce the cost of expensive ones.

# II. Game Components

## 3. Components

**3.1** The game consists of:
- A mapboard (four panels, optional Western Extension panel)
- Nine sets of playing pieces
- 24 civilization cards (eight of each type)
- 114 commodity cards (plus spare blanks); seventy commodity cards in ADVANCED CIVILIZATION
- 12 calamity cards (eight in ADVANCED CIVILIZATION)
- Archaeological Succession Card (A.S.T., Census Track, Trade Card Stacks)
- Eight Player Mats
- Place Cards
- Rulebook and Gamer's Guide
- Summary of Calamity Effects
- Civilization Card Credit Table (back of rulebook)

---

## 4. The Mapboard

**4.1** Four panels forming a stylized map of the ancient Mediterranean world. A fifth Western Extension panel is optional.

**4.2 Areas:**
- **4.21 Land areas:** bounded by white lines with green or brown territory
- **4.22 Water areas:** contain blue; areas with both land and water are both types
- **4.23 Open sea areas:** water areas containing only blue territory
- **4.24 Coastal areas:** contain both land and ocean (non-lake) water

**4.3 Boundaries:**
- **4.31** Land boundaries — white line between two land areas
- **4.32** Water boundaries — white line between two water areas (including lakes)
- **4.33** A boundary crossing both land and water is both. Rules use "all-land" and "all-water" where specific.

**4.4 Geographical features:**
- **4.41** Volcanoes — white triangles; one in the Aegean (one area), two in Sicily/Italy (straddle two areas each)
- **4.42** Flood plains — dark green coloring
- **4.43** City Sites — small black squares; white squares are on flood plains and vulnerable to floods

**4.5** Population limits indicated by a number in a colored circle on each area.

**4.6** Start areas indicated by a colored border matching the nation's token color.

**4.7** The map divider (dotted line, middle panel) divides the board for games with fewer than six players.

---

## 5. Playing Pieces

Each of nine sets contains: 55 large square tokens, 9 round cities, 4 rectangular ships, 1 small square census marker, 1 small square succession marker.

- **5.2 Tokens** — square; represent rural populations. "Token" does not include ships or cities.
- **5.3 Cities** — round; represent urban populations.
- **5.4 Ships** — rectangular; used only to move tokens across water areas.
- **5.5 Units** — tokens and cities, but not ships.
- **5.6 Unit points** — value of tokens and cities: each city = **5 unit points**, each token = **1 unit point**.

## 6. Civilization Cards

**6.1** 24 cards representing important aspects of civilization. Five groups:
- Arts (blue — triangle)
- Crafts (orange — square)
- Sciences (green — circle)
- Civics (red — hexagon)
- Religion (yellow — star)

Four cards belong to two groups each.

**6.2** Where card attributes create exceptions to general rules, those exceptions are printed in *italics*.

## 10. Archaeological Succession Card

**10.1** Three sections: A.S.T., Trade Card Stacks, Census Track.

**10.2** The A.S.T. contains a horizontal track per nation (16 spaces including start arrow and finish square). Divided into five epochs by shading:
- Stone Age
- Early Bronze Age
- Late Bronze Age
- Early Iron Age
- Late Iron Age (each space has a point value; entry requires a minimum civilization card value — see 33.25)

**10.21** Not all nations enter epochs at the same time.

**10.3** Trade Card Stacks hold trade cards during play (nine stacks, one per trade card level).

**10.4** Census Track holds each nation's census marker after population expansion.

---

## 11. Player Mats

**11.1** Hold all tokens, cities and ships not currently in play (**stock**). Pieces removed from the board go to stock and may return. No piece is ever permanently removed from the game.

**11.2** Tokens placed in the **treasury** area become currency for purchasing civilization cards. Keep stock tokens and treasury tokens separate — treasury tokens should be inverted.

---

## 12. Place Cards

Used at game start to determine the order in which players select nations. Put aside once the game begins.

---

## 13. Calamity Effects Summary

A separate sheet setting out the effects of each calamity for ease of reference.

---

## 14. Civilization Card Credit and Game Record Sheet

**14.1** The Credit Table allows quick calculation of accumulated credits towards civilization card purchases.

**14.2** The Game Record Chart lets players record purchases and maintain a running total of civilization card points. Players must reveal this total when asked by another player.

# III. Getting Started

## 15. Setting Up the Game

**15.1** Lay out the mapboard and Archaeological Succession Card. All players place a Player Mat in front of them.

**15.2** Sort civilization cards by type. Sort commodity cards by number, shuffle each stack, then count out one card per player from each stack (stacks 2–9) and set aside. Shuffle each of the eight tradable calamity cards into the remaining commodity cards of the same numeric value. Place the reserved commodity cards on top of their stacks. Place the four non-tradable calamity cards at the **bottom** of their respective stack. Stack 1 gets no calamity.

**15.3** Shuffle place cards and deal one per player. In sequence, each player selects a nation, takes its pieces, and places one token in any of its start areas (4.6).

**15.31** Even with eight players, one nation is unused. Its units serve as Barbarian Hordes and Pirate pieces.

**15.4** Players place succession markers on their nation's start arrow on the A.S.T. and census markers on the Census Track. Play begins.

**15.5 Late arrivals:**
- **15.51** A player may join mid-game by selecting an unused nation and waiting for a Civil War.
- **15.52** On a Civil War, the new player is the beneficiary, gains the Civil War result, plus (at no cost) the same civilization cards and A.S.T. position as the Civil War victim. This only applies when a new player is entering.

**15.6 Early departures:**
- **15.61** The departing player's units remain inert on the board until eliminated. The nation does not move, expand, or require city support. It may not be a secondary calamity victim.
- **15.62** On departure, the nation retains one trade card per city on the board (at random). Excess cards return to stacks. When an inert city is eliminated, the attacker draws one of the retained trade cards and pillages.
- **15.63** The inert nation retains its civilization cards; their attributes apply when resolving attacks against it.

# IV. Turn by Turn Procedure

## 17. The Game Turn

**17.1** Each turn is divided into distinct **phases**. All phases are completed before a new turn begins.

**17.2** Many phases may be carried out simultaneously. Players may insist on prescribed order if another player's actions affect them.

**17.3** The order of player activity varies by phase as detailed in each phase's rules (summarized in the sequence of play).

**17.4** All ties (except conflict) are resolved by A.S.T. order — the order of nations listed on the A.S.T.
